# Hiring Pipeline Analysis & Application Strategy

This document provides the broader strategic context for job searching. The main SKILL.md references this when deeper context is needed on pipeline types, qualification scoring, or application workflow design.

---

## Hiring Pipeline Types

Not all job application channels are equal. The candidate's profile, strengths, and constraints determine which pipelines yield positive expected value and which are time sinks. Understanding this is the foundation of an efficient search.

### Pipeline 1: Corporate ATS → Automated Screen → Panel

Large organizations (Fortune 500, government contractors, enterprise "digital transformation" teams). HR posts JD, ATS filters by keywords, automated coding test or questionnaire, recruiter phone screen, then finally a hiring manager.

Characteristics:
- High applicant volume, high false-negative rate on automated filters
- Coding screens test narrow algorithmic skills that may have no relationship to the actual role
- Cover letters and research are rarely read at the filter stage
- The hiring manager who would appreciate the candidate may never see the application

When to play: Only with a warm referral that bypasses the automated screen. Cold applications to this pipeline are negative expected value for candidates whose strengths are architectural thinking, production judgment, and pattern recognition rather than competitive programming.

### Pipeline 2: Founder/CTO-Led Small Company

Startups and small companies (10-100 people), typically Series A/B. The founder, CTO, or engineering lead reviews applications personally. Technical assessment is a conversation, take-home, or pairing session rather than an automated gauntlet.

Characteristics:
- A human reads the resume and cover letter
- Signal-dense facts on the resume (shipped X to Y users, built Z before it was mainstream) work in 30 seconds
- Company research and tailored cover letters reach the decision-maker
- Technical assessment evaluates how you think, not whether you memorize algorithms

When to play: This is the primary application channel for candidates with strong production experience and architectural depth. 2-3 applications per week using a streamlined 2-3 hour workflow.

Best sources: HN "Who is Hiring" (monthly), Wellfound, Built In, Y Combinator job board.

### Pipeline 3: Community/Reputation → Inbound

You build in public (open source, blog posts, social media, conference talks) → people in the community see your thinking → when they have a role or know of one, they reach out or refer you.

Characteristics:
- The "application" is your body of work and public presence
- Conversion rate is very high once you're visible, but visibility takes time
- The strongest version: someone you've never met recommends you because they've seen your work
- Example: builders who share substantial open-source projects, get noticed, and receive inbound offers

When to play: As a background process. 1-2 substantial posts per week on the platform where your target audience lives. Not the primary strategy on a 1-2 month timeline, but compounds over time. LinkedIn tends to be more generous than Twitter/X for senior engineers with long careers and substantive takes.

### Pipeline 4: Referral / Warm Introduction

Someone in your network introduces you to a hiring manager or vouches for you internally. Consistently the highest-conversion channel — documented success stories at top companies almost always involve a referral or unconventional path.

When to play: Actively map your network — former colleagues who've moved on, course/conference cohort members, professional community contacts. One warm intro to a hiring manager is worth 10 cold applications.

### Pipeline 5: Specialized Recruiters

Senior-level recruiting firms that understand the specific engineering domain and can coach on positioning. Not body shops that keyword-match resumes.

What to look for:
- Specializes in the candidate's domain (e.g., AI/ML engineering placements)
- Willing to have a strategy conversation about positioning
- Has relationships with startups and mid-stage companies, not just enterprise
- Understands that unconventional backgrounds can be strengths
- Open to remote/international candidates if relevant

What to avoid:
- High-volume firms that blast resumes to job boards
- Recruiters who can't discuss the candidate's domain intelligently
- Anyone who wants to flatten the resume into generic corporate language

## 10-Minute Qualification Filter

For any listing, score 0-2 on each dimension. 7+ out of 10 = full application. 5-6 = lightweight application (tailored resume + short cover note). Below 5 = skip.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|-----------|---|---|---|
| **Human Reader** | Large corp, faceless ATS | Mid-size, hiring manager name findable | <100 people, or founder/CTO named in listing |
| **Problem-Shaped JD** | Pure tech stack checklist ("5+ yrs of X") | Mix of problems and requirements | Describes what they're building and why it's hard |
| **No Auto Gauntlet** | Known HackerRank/Codility screen | Unknown screening process | Confirmed conversation-based or take-home process |
| **Core Skill Relevance** | Generic overlap | Some alignment with candidate's key strengths | Role specifically needs what the candidate uniquely offers |
| **Remote + Fresh** | Weekly in-office required or stale listing | Hybrid with periodic travel OK | Primarily remote (quarterly travel fine), posted <2 weeks ago |

## Application Workflow Tiers

### Streamlined Application (2-3 hours) — Default for Pipeline 2

Phase 1: Qualify (10 min)
- Run the 5-dimension filter. If score < 7, stop or downgrade to lightweight.

Phase 2: Quick Research (30 min)
- Company website + recent funding/press (5 min)
- Engineering blog or founder's public writing (10 min)
- LinkedIn: who's the hiring manager? What's their background? (5 min)
- Note 2-3 specific things to reference authentically (10 min)

Phase 3: Tailor Resume (30 min)
- Start from master resume
- Reorder projects by relevance to this role
- Rewrite 3-5 bullet points to mirror the JD's vocabulary
- Adjust skills section ordering

Phase 4: Cover Letter (45 min)
- Opening: One specific reference from quick research
- Middle: 2 projects that map to the role's core need
- Gap acknowledgment if needed (one sentence)
- Close: Genuine, specific, brief
- Target: 300-400 words

Phase 5: Submit + Log (15 min)
- Submit application
- Log: company, role, date, score, channel, follow-up date
- If possible: brief LinkedIn connection note to hiring manager

### Deep Dive Application (6-8 hours) — Reserve for exceptional fits

Only for score 9-10 roles where deep company research, insider references, and a narrative cover letter genuinely add value. This includes extensive research into company leadership, internal initiatives, published engineering work, and culture signals. The output is a cover letter that demonstrates genuine insider knowledge and a carefully constructed narrative arc connecting the candidate's background to the company's specific needs.

Reserve for: roles at companies the candidate would be genuinely thrilled to work at, where there's an almost-perfect match, and where the extra research investment is likely to reach a human who will appreciate it.

### Lightweight Application (30-45 min) — For score 5-6 roles

Tailored resume (reorder projects, adjust skills section) + short cover email (3-4 sentences: who you are, one strong fit point, availability). No deep research. Volume play.

## Weekly Rhythm (for candidates currently employed)

Monday evening (1 hr): Scan new listings. HN Who is Hiring (monthly), LinkedIn saved searches, Built In, Wellfound. Run 10-min filter. Queue qualified roles.

Tuesday-Thursday evenings (2-3 hrs each): Apply to 2-3 qualified roles using the streamlined workflow.

Friday evening (1 hr): Follow up on previous week's applications. LinkedIn connection requests. One LinkedIn post about current work or thinking.

Weekend (optional, 1-2 hrs): Deep-dive application for exceptional roles. Or work on visibility-building (open source, blog post, etc.).

Target: 8-12 applications per month. At 10-15% interview conversion, this yields 1-2 interviews per month.
